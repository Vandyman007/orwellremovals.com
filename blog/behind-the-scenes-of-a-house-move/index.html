Behind the Scenes of a House Move

Get a Free Quote 01473 411531

Moving Guides · Orwell Removals & Storage

When the van pulls away from your old front door and rolls off down the lane, it can look like the easy part is over. In our experience, though, the smoothest moves are the ones where a great deal of quiet work has already happened behind the scenes. As a family-run firm based in Westerfield near Ipswich, we have been planning Suffolk house moves since 2005, and here we lift the lid on everything that goes on before, during and after the day itself.

It Starts Long Before the Van Arrives

People are often surprised to learn how much happens in the weeks before a single box is lifted. A house move is really a logistics exercise wearing the costume of a stressful day, and the groundwork we lay early is what makes the day itself feel calm. From the moment you first get in touch, we are building a picture of your home, your timescales and the things that matter most to you, so nothing is left to chance when the deadline finally arrives.

We have found that the best starting point is always a proper conversation about the property. How many rooms, how many floors, whether there is a loft full of memories or a garage stacked with tools, and crucially how easy it is to get a van close to the door. A terrace with no parking and a shared alley needs a very different plan from a detached house with a sweeping drive, and knowing that in advance lets us bring the right team and the right kit.

From those early notes we work out the size of vehicle, the number of crew and the running order for the day. Since 2005 we have learned that a move planned around the real shape of a home, rather than a rough guess, is one that finishes on time and without drama. That planning is invisible to most customers, but it is the foundation everything else is built upon.

The Survey and the Quote

Before we ever commit to a date, we like to understand exactly what we are moving. Sometimes that means a visit to walk through the rooms with you, and sometimes a video call around the house works just as well for a smaller property. Either way, we are looking for the things that catch people out: the wardrobe that will not fit down the stairs in one piece, the piano nobody mentioned, the shed at the bottom of the garden that is somehow also coming.

This is also the moment we talk honestly about access at both ends. A second-floor flat in central Ipswich with a tight stairwell asks more of a crew than a bungalow, and a narrow rural track that a large lorry cannot reach changes the whole approach. By spotting these details early we can give you a quote that reflects the real job, rather than a hopeful figure that grows on the day. We would always rather tell you the truth up front than surprise you later.

The quote that lands in your inbox is the visible tip of a good deal of thinking. Behind it sits a judgement about volume, time, crew, vehicles and any special handling, all weighed up from years of moving Suffolk families. We want it to be fair to you and workable for us, because a quote that is too tight helps nobody when the day runs long.

Packing Day and the Quiet Art of Preparation

For many of our customers the packing is the part they dread most, and it is often where we save them the most worry. Whether you pack yourself or ask our crews to do it, the principle is the same: every box should be sensible to lift, clearly labelled and packed so nothing rattles. We bring proper materials, plenty of paper and the kind of boxes that hold their shape under the weight of a fully loaded van.

When our team packs a home, there is a rhythm to it that comes from long practice. We tend to work room by room, finishing one space completely before moving on, so the house empties in an orderly way rather than descending into chaos. Kitchens and fragile items get the slow, careful treatment, while books and linens move quickly. Labelling as we go, with the destination room marked clearly, is what makes unpacking at the other end so much faster.

Behind the scenes we are also protecting the house itself. Doorways get padded where furniture has to squeeze through, floors are covered on wet Suffolk mornings, and bannisters are guarded against knocks. None of this is glamorous, and most customers never notice it, but it is the difference between handing back a tidy home and leaving scuffs behind. Preparation like this is the unsung craft of a good move.

Loading the Van: Tetris With a Purpose

Loading a removals van well is a genuine skill, and watching an experienced crew do it is a little like watching a puzzle solve itself. There is a deliberate order to it. Heavy, sturdy furniture goes in first to form a stable base, then boxes are tiered and wedged so the load becomes one solid block that cannot shift on the road. Gaps are the enemy, because a gap is where things slide and bump on the journey.

We protect as we load, not as an afterthought. Sofas and mattresses are wrapped, table tops are blanketed, and corners that might rub are padded. Fragile boxes are kept clear of anything that could crush them, and we make a point of loading so that whatever is needed first at the new house comes off the van last. That little touch means the kettle and the children's beds are not buried behind a wall of wardrobes when you arrive.

All of this is happening while we keep one eye on the clock and the other on the weather, because a Suffolk morning can turn from bright to drizzly in minutes. A well-loaded van is safer to drive, kinder to your belongings and quicker to empty at the other end. The crew make it look effortless, but every decision about what goes where has a reason behind it.

On the Road and Into the New Home

The drive between homes is the calmest stretch of the day for you, but our crews are still working. We choose sensible routes that suit a loaded vehicle, keep speeds gentle so the load stays settled, and stay in touch about timings so you are not left guessing when we will arrive. For longer moves out of the county we plan stops and check the load is still secure along the way.

Arriving at the new house, the first thing we do is take stock of the access and the layout before a single item comes off the van. A quick walk through tells us where each room is, where the tricky corners hide and whether the same wardrobe that came down the old stairs will go up the new ones. Then the unload begins in reverse order, with boxes and furniture going straight to the room marked on the label rather than piling up by the front door.

We will happily reassemble beds, set wardrobes back together and place the heavy pieces where you want them, so you are not wrestling a divan alone at ten at night. Throughout the move your belongings are fully covered while they are in our hands, which is the reassurance that lets you focus on the exciting part of settling in. Our aim is always to leave you in a home you can start living in, not a maze of boxes.

After We Have Gone: The Part You Do Not See

The job is not quite finished when the van doors close for the last time. Back at our Westerfield base there is a routine that keeps the next move running as smoothly as yours did. Vehicles are cleaned and checked, blankets and straps are sorted and any equipment that needs attention is dealt with before it goes out again. A well-kept fleet is part of why we have been able to keep serving Suffolk reliably since 2005.

We also take a moment to reflect on how each move went. If a particular property threw up a challenge, that knowledge feeds into how we plan the next one like it. Over nearly two decades these small lessons have built up into the practical know-how that lets us look at a home and know roughly how the day will unfold. It is experience you cannot buy off a shelf, only earn one move at a time.

For some customers our involvement carries on after move day through storage, whether that is a gap between completion dates or simply somewhere to keep belongings while a new home is decorated. As a firm offering both removals and storage, we can hold things securely and bring them out when you are ready, so a tricky chain or a slow renovation need not derail your plans. That continuity, from first enquiry to final box, is what being a family-run local firm is really about.
