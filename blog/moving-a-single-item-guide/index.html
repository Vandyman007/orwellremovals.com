Moving a Single Large Item: A Quick Guide

Get a Free Quote 01473 411531

Moving Guides · Orwell Removals & Storage

Not every move is a whole house. Sometimes it is one stubborn, heavy, awkward item that simply will not fit in the car, and getting it from A to B without scratching the walls or hurting your back is its own small art. Whether it is a sofa, a fridge-freezer, a wardrobe or a treasured antique, here is how our Suffolk crews approach moving a single large item, and how you can plan it properly.

When You Just Need One Thing Moved

Single-item moves come up far more often than people expect, and they are a genuine specialism in their own right. Maybe you have bought a sofa from a marketplace and the seller cannot deliver, or inherited a wardrobe from a relative, or need to shift a piano to a new room, or take a fridge-freezer to a holiday let. The item is too big for the car, too heavy for two friends and a Saturday afternoon, and too valuable or too awkward to risk damaging. That is exactly the kind of job we handle.

The temptation is to muddle through with a borrowed van and a couple of mates, and sometimes that works out fine. But heavy single items are deceptively risky. They are unwieldy, often go up or down stairs, and a single slip can damage the item, the walls, the doorframes or, worst of all, someone's back. We've been called out plenty of times to rescue a sofa wedged on a staircase or finish a job that started cheerfully and ended badly.

Booking a proper single-item move takes all of that worry away. You get the right vehicle, the right number of people, the right equipment and the experience to move the thing smoothly, often in a fraction of the time it would take to improvise. For one important item, that peace of mind is usually well worth it.

Measuring Up Before You Move

The single biggest cause of single-item disasters is not measuring first, and it is entirely avoidable. Before anything is lifted, measure the item itself, its height, width and depth, and then measure every doorway, hallway, staircase and turn it has to pass through at both ends. Pay particular attention to tight turns at the top or bottom of stairs, where a long item has to be angled, and to doorways that look generous until a wardrobe is halfway through them.

Do not forget the route in between, including any low ceilings, light fittings, banisters and handrails that might be in the way. If the item is going into a flat, check whether it will fit in the lift, and if not, whether the stairwell can take it. We always carry out these checks as part of a single-item job, because the worst possible moment to discover something will not fit is when four people are holding it on a landing. A few minutes with a tape measure beforehand saves a great deal of grief.

If the measurements are tight, there are usually options: removing a door from its hinges, taking the legs off a sofa, dismantling a wardrobe, or in rare cases bringing an item in through a window with the right equipment. Knowing the dimensions in advance lets us plan the right approach calmly, rather than improvising on the day with the item stuck halfway.

Protecting the Item and Your Home

Moving one item well means protecting both the item and everything it passes. We wrap furniture in proper removal blankets, secure them with straps, and use corner protection on anything with edges that could catch or chip. Upholstered pieces get covered to keep them clean, and anything with glass or delicate finishes is padded thoroughly. The goal is that the item arrives in exactly the condition it left, with not a mark on it.

Your home matters just as much. Squeezing a heavy item through a house is where scuffed walls, chipped paint and dented skirting boards happen, so we protect the route as well as the item. That can mean covering floors, padding doorframes and tight corners, and lifting rather than dragging at every stage. We've found that taking a few extra minutes to protect the property properly is always quicker in the end than dealing with damage, and it keeps everyone happy.

Lifting technique is the other half of protection, the half that protects people. Heavy items should be lifted with the legs, kept close to the body, and never twisted under load. Our crews are trained and practised at this, and we use the right equipment, straps, trolleys and sliders, to take the strain. Moving a heavy single item is precisely the kind of job where doing it the hard way risks an injury that lasts far longer than the move.

Special Care for Awkward Items

Some single items need more than careful lifting; they need genuine know-how. A piano is the classic example, an extremely heavy, delicate and valuable instrument that should never be tackled by amateurs, and one we treat with specialist care. But plenty of other items are awkward in their own way: large mirrors and glass cabinets that must not flex, marble-topped tables that are heavier and more fragile than they look, American-style fridge-freezers that barely fit through doors, and antiques whose value makes any damage especially painful.

For each of these, the approach is tailored to the item. Glass and mirrors are wrapped, supported and kept upright so they cannot crack. Heavy stone tops are carried on edge by enough people to take the weight safely. Appliances are moved upright and given time to settle before being switched on. Antiques are handled with extra padding and slower, more deliberate movements, and where they need dismantling, it is done carefully so they go back together exactly as they should.

The key with any awkward item is to tell us about it when you book, with the dimensions and any quirks, so we can bring the right equipment and the right number of crew. We would much rather know in advance that we are collecting a marble table or a glass display cabinet than discover it on arrival. Forewarned, we can move almost anything; surprised, even a simple job gets harder than it should.

Booking and Planning the Job

Planning a single-item move is refreshingly simple if you give us the right information up front. Tell us what the item is, its approximate dimensions and weight, where it is collecting from and where it is going, and what the access is like at both ends, stairs, lifts, parking and any tight turns. The more we know, the more accurately we can quote and the more smoothly the job runs. A clear photo of the item and the access points is often worth a hundred words.

Because a single item is a smaller job, it is usually quick and cost-effective, and we can often fit it in flexibly around the calendar. Be ready for us when we arrive: clear the route of obstacles, move smaller furniture out of the way, secure pets and make sure someone is available to let us in and confirm where the item is going. A few minutes of preparation lets the crew get straight to work and keeps the job short.

If the single item is going somewhere you are not ready to receive it yet, that is no problem either. We can collect it and keep it in secure storage until you want it delivered, which is handy if you have bought something ahead of a move or a renovation. Just mention it when you book and we will plan the collection, storage and delivery as one easy arrangement, so your new sofa or wardrobe is safe and waiting whenever you are ready for it.

Combining a Single Item With Other Jobs

Single-item moves do not have to happen in isolation, and there is often sense in combining them with other tasks. If you are already moving home, that one extra piece coming from a relative's house can be folded into the main removal rather than booked separately. Equally, a single-item collection can be paired with a trip to or from storage, or with clearing a few other bits, so you get more done in one efficient visit rather than several.

We've found that customers often start with a single item and realise there is a little more they would like moved while we are there, and that is absolutely fine. Because we bring the right vehicle and crew, adding a couple of extra pieces is usually straightforward, though it helps to mention it when you book so we bring a vehicle with enough room and allow the time. Springing a wardrobe on us when we arrive for a sofa is the only thing that catches us out.

Whether it stays a single item or grows into a small move, the principle is the same: tell us what you need, give us the dimensions and access details, and let us plan it properly. A single large item is exactly the sort of job where experience, the right kit and a bit of forethought turn a potential headache into a quick, clean, stress-free piece of work, with your belongings and your home protected throughout.
