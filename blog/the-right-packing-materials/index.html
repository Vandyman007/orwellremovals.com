The Right Packing Materials for a Move

Get a Free Quote 01473 411531

Packing · Orwell Removals & Storage

Good packing materials are the unsung heroes of a smooth move. The wrong box splits halfway to the van; the right one carries your kitchen safely across the county without a wobble. After two decades packing Suffolk homes from our Westerfield base, our crews have firm views on what actually works and what's a waste of money. This is a plain, practical guide to the materials worth having, how much you'll need, and where people most often get it wrong.

Boxes: Get the Sizes and Strength Right

Boxes are where most of your packing budget goes, and choosing well repays you many times over. The mistake we see again and again is one giant box for everything. Instead, you want a mix of sizes for different jobs. Small boxes are for heavy things like books, records and tinned food; large boxes are for light, bulky items such as bedding, cushions and soft toys. Match the size to the weight and lifting becomes safe and sensible.

Strength matters as much as size. Double-walled boxes, with their thicker corrugated construction, resist crushing when stacked in the van and are well worth it for anything heavy or fragile. Single-walled boxes are fine for lighter contents. Whatever you use, the box needs a flat base and four intact flaps; a tired box that's been through three previous moves is a false economy when it gives way at the worst moment.

As a rough guide, an average three-bedroom Suffolk home needs more boxes than people ever expect, so it's better to have a few spare than to run short on packing night. Specialist boxes earn their place too, which we'll come to. If you'd rather not guess, our team can advise on quantities and supply sturdy boxes, so you start with the right kit rather than a stack of supermarket cast-offs.

Wrapping and Cushioning Materials

What goes around your belongings inside the box is just as important as the box itself. Plain packing paper is our everyday workhorse. It wraps, it cushions, and crucially it doesn't transfer ink onto pale china and glassware the way newspaper does. Buy more than you think you need, because it gets used up astonishingly fast once you start wrapping a kitchen's worth of crockery.

Bubble wrap is the material to reach for with anything genuinely fragile or precious. It's perfect for glasses, ornaments, electronics and the corners of framed pictures, giving a springy cushion that absorbs knocks. You don't need to swaddle everything in it, though; using bubble wrap only where it counts and paper for the rest keeps costs sensible without compromising on protection.

Don't overlook the soft furnishings you already own. Tea towels, jumpers, bedding and towels make excellent free padding, filling gaps in boxes and protecting items in the same trip. We often tuck a folded towel around a casserole dish or use a duvet to cushion a box of glassware. It saves money, saves materials and means your soft items travel usefully rather than taking up a box of their own.

Tape, Markers and the Small Essentials

It's the little things that hold a move together, quite literally. Strong packing tape is non-negotiable; the thin, cheap kind peels off in the cold or splits under weight, and there's nothing more dispiriting than a box bursting open in the hallway. Buy proper packing tape and, ideally, a tape gun, which makes sealing dozens of boxes far quicker and gives a cleaner, stronger seal along every seam.

Tape the base of each box before you fill it, running a strip down the central seam and one across each end. This reinforced base is what stops the bottom dropping out when you lift a full box. The same goes for the top once it's packed. A well-taped box keeps its shape, stacks neatly and protects its contents far better than one held shut with a single hopeful strip.

A couple of bold marker pens are essential for labelling rooms and contents, and coloured stickers or paper make a colour-coding system easy to set up. Keep a pair of scissors or a craft knife handy, plus some resealable bags for screws and small fittings when you dismantle furniture. These cost very little but save real frustration, so gather them before you start rather than hunting mid-pack.

Specialist Materials for Tricky Items

Some belongings simply ask for purpose-made materials, and they're worth seeking out. Wardrobe boxes are a brilliant example: tall boxes with a hanging rail inside, they let clothes travel straight from the rail on their hangers and back onto the rail at the other end. No folding, no creasing, no ironing marathon when you arrive, just lift and hang. For a family with a lot of clothes, they transform the bedroom packing.

Picture and mirror boxes are flat, often adjustable, and protect framed items far better than trying to wedge them into a standard box. Mattress covers keep beds clean through a dusty van and a muddy doorway, and they're cheap insurance against a stain on something you sleep on. For dismantled furniture, corner protectors and furniture blankets guard against scuffs and dents on the journey.

For dishes and glassware, divided box inserts create individual cells that hold each piece apart, which makes packing a full kitchen of stemware genuinely quick and safe. You won't need every specialist item for every move, but matching a few to your particular home, lots of clothes, a fragile dinner service, a treasured painting, pays off. Our crews carry these materials, so if you'd rather not source them yourself, just ask.

How Much Will You Actually Need?

One of the questions our team is asked most often is simply how much to buy, and getting it roughly right saves both money and last-minute panic. The honest answer is that people almost always underestimate, because a home holds far more than it appears to until you start boxing it up. The kitchen alone, with its crockery, glassware and cupboard contents, swallows a surprising number of boxes once everything is wrapped.

As a sensible approach, work room by room and picture what's in each, then add a margin on top, since running out of boxes on packing night is a genuine nuisance. Bedrooms need wardrobe boxes plus general boxes for the contents of drawers and shelves; the living room needs sturdy boxes for books and media; the loft, shed and garage almost always need more than you'd think. It's far better to have a couple of spare boxes than to be improvising with bin bags at eleven at night.

Materials run out faster than boxes, too. A single kitchen can use a whole roll of packing paper, so buy generously. Rather than guess, you're welcome to talk it through with us; after two decades of packing Suffolk homes our crews can give a realistic estimate for a property your size, so you order the right amount without over-buying or falling short on the night.

Eco-Friendly Choices and Saving Money

You don't have to choose between protecting your belongings and being sensible about cost or waste, and we encourage customers to think about both. Reusing boxes is the obvious win, provided they're still strong, with sound bases and clean flaps. Boxes from a previous move, sturdy ones passed on by friends, or returnable crates all cut both spending and cardboard, and they pack just as well as new ones if they're in good shape.

Lean on what you already own before you buy. Suitcases and holdalls are perfect for heavy items and travel well; laundry baskets and storage tubs carry plenty; and your own towels, bedding and clothes do the cushioning work that bubble wrap might otherwise do. A move is also a natural moment to declutter, so anything you decide not to keep is one less box to buy, fill and carry.

When you do buy materials, buy the right amount rather than the cheapest, since under-packing to save a few pounds tends to cost far more in breakages. After the move, flatten and keep your best boxes for next time, or pass them on so they get another life. If you'd like guidance on exactly what your move needs without over-ordering, our team is always happy to talk it through before a box is bought.
